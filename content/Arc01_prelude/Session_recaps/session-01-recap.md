## Quick Summary

The first session opened in **Amble**, a small frontier village where everyone knows everyone else’s business and reputation matters nearly as much as truth. The children began as young, untrained village kids with more nerve than sense, and their first real trouble came quickly: **Bram Cotter**, older and larger than them, had cornered **Wren** near the council tree and was demanding the return of a small carved wooden charm made by his grandfather.

The confrontation turned ugly. Bram shouted for the charm, Wren insisted she did not have it, and the children stepped in before the anger became something worse. A messy brawl followed — shoves, dust, bruises, torn clothes, and children discovering that bravery does not make them good at fighting. The group lost more than they won, but Wren escaped the immediate threat.

At the hidden fort / cubby house, Wren admitted the truth: she had borrowed Bram’s charm to draw it and accidentally lost it. The children found the charm, but instead of simply returning it, they turned a small problem into a much larger one. They tried to frame **Nell Weaver**, wrapped the charm inside a misleading note, and sent **Moss Pebblefoot** to deliver it. The delivery went badly: Moss threw the wrapped parcel at Bram’s house, missed the window, hit the front wall, and ran. The next day, the note ended up back in **Fenwick’s** house, where his mother saw through the lie.

Meanwhile, the village was also worried about sheep being killed or going missing near the bridge and woods. Wren had seen a fox-like creature nearby. When the children investigated at dusk, they found a hollow tree glowing faint blue in the dark. Inside was a small injured glowing fox-like creature with sheep remains nearby. The creature’s pain struck the children mentally — a raw, frightening brush with something unnatural. Rather than hand it over to adults, the group hid it in their fort, lied to Mother Yela to get balm, and nursed it back toward health.

During its recovery, secrecy frayed. Elsie Brook had already caught the group in the woods, and during the following week she showed the fox to Nell Weaver, widening the circle of people who knew about the creature. Mother Yela also discovered the balm lie and set the children to work off the value — **3 silver** — across the summer holidays.

Later in spring, during a village celebration, **Marek Voss** arrived with medicines and supplies for Mother Yela. His visit pulled the first Guild-shaped shadow across the story: shortages to the north and west, sickness in other villages, and a farmer demanding news of a son who had joined the Guild in connection with debt. The session ended with the sense that childhood trouble in Amble was only the beginning. Years would pass; the children would grow older; Wren’s mother’s health would seriously decline.

## What Happened

### Amble, the Children, and the Council Tree

Amble began as a small working village of roughly 300 people, close enough to the great forest for danger to feel real but ordinary enough that the children still thought in terms of chores, sweets, and who would get in trouble with whose parents. The village has a council tree near its centre, a hidden fort / cubby house known to the children, and nearby fields where sheep are a meaningful part of village life.

The children were still very young. They had no formal training, no heroic equipment, and little practical authority. Their early choices showed a mix of courage, panic, loyalty, and terrible judgement. That tone defined much of the session: the children repeatedly tried to do the right thing, but almost always made the situation more complicated.

Near the council tree, Bram Cotter had Wren cornered. He was older, broader, and angry. Wren was backed against the tree, trying to hold her nerve while Bram demanded his missing charm. His accusation was simple and loud: he believed she had taken it.

Key confrontation beats:

- Bram demanded the charm back.
- Wren insisted she did not have it.
- Other children were watching and egging things on.
- Bram raised a fist and looked ready to hurt her.
- The player characters stepped in rather than walk away.

Memorable table-facing version of the confrontation:

> “Give it back now.”
>
> “I don’t have it.”
>
> “You’re a liar.”

That exchange set the social fault line for the session: Bram had adult approval and village sympathy; Wren had fear, suspicion, and very few people willing to believe her.

### The Brawl

The children tried to intervene. Some moved between Bram and Wren; some tried to grab him; some swung at him. The result was not a clean heroic rescue but a chaotic children’s fight. Bram shoved them around, other boys joined in, and the children discovered that standing up to a bully did not mean they could easily beat him.

The fight was rough and embarrassing rather than glorious. There were missed swings, failed grapples, shouting children, bruises, torn clothes, and more confidence than competence. Some of the children were knocked down, and the adults later heard about the fight almost immediately because in Amble every child went home and told someone.

Important outcome:

- Wren got away from Bram’s immediate threat.
- Bram was humiliated and angered.
- The player children gained a reputation for getting involved in trouble.
- Several adults later treated Bram as sympathetic and Wren/the party as suspicious.

### Adults Hear About the Fight

After the fight, the children tried to explain themselves at home. Their explanations did not go well. One attempted story involved getting run over by sheep. Another version tried to make the council tree fight sound like something else. Adults already knew enough from village gossip to see through the lies.

This established several recurring social facts:

- The village communication network is fast.
- The children are not nearly as good at lying to adults as they think.
- Adults often interpret Bram generously.
- Adults often interpret Wren negatively.
- The children’s parents already see some of the group as bad influences on each other.

### Wren at the Hidden Fort

The group later found Wren at their hidden fort / cubby house. She was frightened and apologetic, but not innocent in the simple way the children first hoped. She had taken the charm — not to steal it permanently, but to draw it.

Wren explained that the charm was a small wooden piece on a leather thong, made by Bram’s grandfather. She had borrowed it to look at it and draw it. She meant to return it, but she lost it after hanging it on a tree to keep it safe while cleaning sticky hands.

Important Wren confession beats:

- She did take the charm, but not maliciously.
- She was scared because she knew how important it was to Bram.
- She understood Bram had reason to be upset, but not to hurt her.
- Her drawing helped the group know what they were looking for.
- The group found the charm after searching.

This turned the problem from “Bram falsely accused Wren” into something messier: Wren had made a mistake, Bram had responded cruelly, and the children now had both the truth and the charm.

### The Overcomplicated Charm Scheme

A simple solution existed: give Bram the charm back.

The children did not choose the simple solution.

Instead, they discussed a series of increasingly ridiculous plans. These included sneaking the charm into Bram’s house, planting it among his brother’s things, leaving it somewhere to make Bram look foolish, blaming Nell Weaver, blaming a dog, using the dog as part of the delivery, and generally trying to convince Bram that the charm had been somewhere else all along.

Representative table moments:

> “We’ll just gaslight him.”
>
> “Feed it to the dog.”
>
> “We’re eight.”
>
> “This is a secret mission.”

The final plan involved wrapping the charm inside a note designed to frame **Nell Weaver** for the theft. This mattered because Nell had been connected to the earlier accusation that Wren had possessed the charm. The children hoped the note would push suspicion away from themselves and Wren.

Fenwick’s role became especially important here. He helped create the note, but realised too late that the plan was flawed: the paper came from his own house, and his handwriting was far more practiced than Nell’s would be. He said nothing at the time. The plan was already moving.

### Moss Pebblefoot’s Botched Delivery

Moss Pebblefoot was sent on the delivery run while the others waited near the bridge. The plan was to get the charm and note to Bram’s house without being seen or traced.

It did not go cleanly.

Moss approached Bram’s house, found that the family appeared to be home, and attempted a very indirect delivery. He threw the wrapped parcel toward the house, missed the intended window, hit the front wall with a noticeable clunk, and ran.

Important result:

- Delivery was **not confirmed successful that night**.
- The group did not know whether Bram would get the charm cleanly.
- The note was traceable in ways Fenwick had already begun to fear.
- The attempt to frame Nell Weaver became a live consequence, not just a joke.

### The Note Returns to Fenwick’s House

The next day, the framing note ended up back at Fenwick’s house. Fenwick saw a familiar piece of paper on the table and immediately understood the danger. He tried to get rid of it by dumping it out the window.

His mother caught him.

She read the note, saw through the attempt to frame Nell Weaver almost immediately, and pressed Fenwick for the truth. Fenwick admitted to finding the charm and trying to return it without looking responsible. His explanations did not fully protect the group.

Important consequence:

- **Fenwick’s mother knows** the group recovered the charm.
- She knows they tried to frame Nell Weaver.
- She suspects Moss, Wren, Fergus, and the others were involved.
- The cover-up failed inside Fenwick’s own house.

### Sheep Trouble Near the Village

While the charm scheme was unfolding, adults in Amble were worried about sheep being killed or going missing. The attacks seemed close enough to the village to make people uneasy. Some adults thought wolves, foxes, or other animals might be involved.

Wren said she had seen a small fox-like creature near the bridge. She suggested that finding or trapping it might help the children get back into the village’s good books.

This gave the group a new problem to solve and a new way to make things worse.

### The Bridge and the Blue Glow

The children arranged to meet near the bridge around sunset. Their original reasons were tangled together: investigate the sheep attacks, maybe prove themselves useful, maybe distract from the charm mess, and maybe find out whether Wren’s fox-like creature was real.

The investigation led them away from the bridge and toward the woods. There, in the growing dark, they found signs leading to a hollow tree. A faint blue glow leaked from it.

The scene was strange in a way Amble usually was not. The village had mud, farms, sheep, chores, parents, and gossip. This was different: cold blue light in a tree hollow, darkness pressing in, and the possibility that the thing eating sheep was no normal fox.

### Elsie Brook Catches Them

Elsie Brook was not originally invited into the secret. She emerged from the darkness behind the group after seeing Moss acting suspiciously near Bram’s house and following him all the way to the woods.

She had caught them rather than been invited.

The group tried to bind her to secrecy with an elaborate fake curse and handshake — half joke, half desperate damage control. It worked enough to keep the moment moving, but it also established Elsie as observant and capable. She had noticed what the others missed and followed them into the dark.

Important Elsie facts:

- She knows about the secret mission.
- She knows the group was near Bram’s house.
- She knows about the blue-glowing creature.
- She is connected to Nell Weaver and not guaranteed to keep secrets forever.

### The Glowing Fox-Like Creature

Inside the hollow, the children found a small glowing fox-like creature. It had sheep or lamb remains nearby, linking it to the attacks, but it also looked badly hurt. Its leg appeared mangled, likely from a snapping trap, and it seemed frightened, starving, and in pain.

Elsie thought it looked possibly fey. The others were less certain. Some worried it was dangerous; others wanted to help it. Moss in particular pushed toward helping the creature rather than killing or abandoning it.

When the creature woke, the moment became the children’s first real brush with something supernatural. It growled, panicked, and its fear hit them mentally. It was not just a sound. For a moment, its pain and terror pressed into their heads like a blow: alien, raw, and impossible to mistake for ordinary animal fear.

The children staggered through that moment. The fox was not simply a wounded animal anymore; it was something that could make them feel its agony.

### Cake, Trust, and the Decision to Hide It

The children tried to calm the creature. Wren’s cake became the bridge. A small piece of food, offered carefully, helped reduce the creature’s panic. It accepted some from the children’s hands and allowed itself to be coaxed out.

Moss wanted to help it properly. Bringing it to Mother Yela would have made sense if the children had been able to tell the truth. But they could not explain why they were in the woods at night, what they had been doing before, or how this connected to the sheep attacks and the charm scheme.

So secrecy won.

They carried the injured fox-like creature back to the hidden fort and hid it there with food, water, and whatever care they could manage. The fort, already a place for childhood secrets, became a shelter for something stranger.

### Mother Yela’s Balm

The next day, the children needed medicine for the creature’s injured leg. They went to **Mother Yela**, the village healer, and claimed they needed balm for a dog caught in a trap.

The lie held just long enough to get the balm.

Mother Yela valued the balm at **3 silver** and expected payment through the supposed farmer whose dog had been treated. When she later checked the story, it collapsed. The named farmer’s dogs were fine, and no dog had been caught in a trap.

Mother Yela confronted the children. They tried more explanations, but she saw through enough of it. Rather than immediately expose everything to their parents, she set them to work off the debt across the **entire summer holidays**.

Important consequence:

- Mother Yela knows the children lied to obtain medicine.
- She knows they used it for some hidden animal-related purpose.
- The children owe summer work worth 3 silver.
- Their pattern of choosing secrecy over asking adults for help became more established.

### The Fox Recovers

Over the next week or two, the glowing fox-like creature recovered at the hidden fort. Moss formed a particular bond with it. The group fed it, cared for it, and tried to keep its existence quiet.

The secret widened anyway. Elsie Brook, already part of the secret, took an opportunity to show the recovering fox to **Nell Weaver**, who was impressed.

This mattered. Nell was no longer just the child the group tried to frame for the charm. She now also knew about the fox. The group’s two biggest secrets had both touched her name.

Eventually the fox-like creature left. It sent a mental impression of thanks and suggested it would return. Whether that promise was true, and what it meant, remained uncertain.

### Spring Festival and Marek Voss

Later in spring, Amble held a smaller seasonal celebration. Tables were set out in the village square, the inn was open, and visitors came through town.

Among them was **Marek Voss**, a travelling Guild man who brought medicines and supplies for Mother Yela. He did not arrive like a grand villain or marching officer. He was practical, ordinary, and tied to the supply lines that villages like Amble quietly relied on.

Mother Yela complained that Marek had brought fewer supplies than usual. Marek said things were difficult to the north and west. Some sort of sickness was affecting villages, and people were storing supplies or refusing to sell what they normally would.

Then the Guild thread became personal. A farmer confronted Marek, demanding news of his son — one of the farm boys connected to the family the children had already name-dropped during their balm lie. The son had apparently joined the Guild the previous year in connection with a family debt and had not been heard from.

Marek’s answer gave little comfort: once someone joined the Guild, he could not freely discuss them. In a small village, that kind of answer leaves a mark.

The children learned that the Guild hires out services such as guards or mercenaries, and that families may settle debts by sending people into Guild service. The Guild was no longer an abstract organisation; it had touched a family from Amble.

### Four-Year Gap Setup

After the spring events, several years passed before the next stage of the story. The children grew older, and the events of that first strange summer became part of their shared past.

Wren’s mother grew seriously ill during the intervening years. Her health declined enough to cast a long shadow over Wren and the group’s memories of childhood.

By the next stage, the children are older, the village is not quite the same, and the innocence of the council tree, the hidden fort, and the glowing fox belongs to a younger version of them.

## Important Characters

### Wren

Wren is a quiet tiefling child who is not fully part of the main group at first, but she sometimes comes to their hidden fort. She is small, cautious, and closely associated with drawing and cakes. Her sketching mattered directly in this session because she borrowed Bram’s charm to draw it.

Bram Cotter accused Wren of stealing his wooden charm. Wren had taken it, but without intending to keep it. She borrowed it, drew it, hung it on a tree to keep it safe, and lost track of it. Her mistake triggered the confrontation at the council tree.

Wren also becomes socially important because adults in the village seem ready to believe the worst of her. The children, however, increasingly see her as someone being treated unfairly.

Wren helped calm the glowing fox-like creature by providing cake. Her cake became one of the small gestures that let the frightened creature trust the group.

Wren mentioned that her mother tired easily and had struggled since Wren’s father’s death. By the four-year gap, Wren’s mother’s health has seriously declined.

### Bram Cotter

Bram Cotter is an older and larger village child. He is physically intimidating to the younger children and angry over the loss of his grandfather’s wooden charm. He confronts Wren harshly near the council tree and becomes the spark for the session’s first major conflict.

Bram is not simply treated as “the villain” by the village. Adults tend to see him as a helpful boy who has had a hard life. That social protection makes him more dangerous to the children, because even when they believe he is bullying Wren, adults are inclined to excuse or defend him.

The fight, the lost charm, and the attempted cover-up all worsen Bram’s relationship with the group.

### Moss Pebblefoot

Moss Pebblefoot introduced himself near the council tree. He tends toward direct physical action and was one of the children willing to step in against Bram. Later, he was also the one sent on the risky delivery run to return the charm wrapped in the note framing Nell Weaver.

Moss botched the delivery by throwing the parcel at Bram’s house, missing the window, hitting the wall, and running. This did not confirm the charm had been safely returned.

Moss also became especially attached to the injured glowing fox-like creature. He wanted to help it rather than kill it or abandon it, and he formed the clearest bond with it during its recovery.

### Fenwick

Fenwick is clever, anxious, and prone to complicated schemes. He was part of the group’s efforts to protect Wren, recover the charm, and investigate the strange creature.

Fenwick’s most important contribution this session was the note scheme. He helped create or handle the framing note aimed at Nell Weaver, then realised too late that the paper and handwriting could trace back to him. When the note appeared at his house, he tried to dispose of it by throwing it out the window, but his mother caught him.

Fenwick’s mother saw through the lie and forced him to admit that the group had found the charm. This makes Fenwick’s household one of the first adult leak points in the group’s web of secrets.

### Fergus

Fergus was part of the group’s efforts during the fight, the hidden fort discussions, the charm mess, and the fox events. He often follows the momentum of the group once a plan is in motion, even when the plan is bad.

He was also caught up in the adults’ suspicion after the council tree fight and the later cover-up attempts.

### Elsie Brook

Elsie Brook is observant and harder to keep out of things than the group expected. She followed Moss after seeing him acting suspiciously near Bram’s house and found the group in the woods.

Elsie was not invited into the fox secret; she caught them. The group tried to bind her to secrecy through a fake curse and handshake, but this was more panic than reliable security.

Elsie also helped interpret the fox-like creature. She noticed it seemed injured and possibly fey. Later, while the creature was recovering, she showed it to Nell Weaver, widening the secret beyond the core group.

### Nell Weaver

Nell Weaver is another village child who became important because of the group’s schemes. The children used her as the scapegoat in their note about the missing charm, attempting to frame her for the theft.

Nell also became connected to the fox secret when Elsie Brook showed her the recovering creature. This means Nell now has two reasons to matter later: she was framed in the charm incident, and she knows something about the glowing fox-like creature.

### Mother Yela

Mother Yela is the village healer / medicine woman. She provides medicine, treats practical problems, and is not easily fooled.

The children lied to her to obtain balm for what they claimed was an injured dog. The balm was actually for the glowing fox-like creature. Mother Yela valued the balm at 3 silver, later checked the children’s story, discovered the named farmer’s dog was fine, and confronted them.

Rather than immediately taking everything to their parents, Mother Yela set the children to work off the debt over the entire summer holidays. She now knows they lied to her for an animal-related reason, even if she does not know the full truth of the fox.

### Marek Voss

Marek Voss is a travelling Guild man who visited Amble during the spring celebration. He brings medicines and supplies for Mother Yela and appears to be part of the network Amble depends on for goods it cannot source locally.

His visit introduced several wider-world concerns: shortages to the north and west, sickness affecting villages, reluctance to sell supplies, and the Guild’s connection to debt and service.

A farmer confronted Marek about his son, who had joined the Guild the previous year in connection with a family debt and had not been heard from. Marek’s inability or refusal to say much about the son made the Guild feel more ominous and personal.

## Ominous Threads

### Bram Cotter’s Pride

Bram has his charm back, or at least the group believes it was returned, but his pride has been damaged. He was challenged in front of other children, fought by younger kids, and pulled into a mess that adults still mostly interpret in his favour. A bully with wounded pride and adult sympathy can become dangerous in quieter ways.

### Wren’s Place in Amble

Wren made a mistake, but the village was ready to believe the worst of her. The children saw the gap between what happened and what adults wanted to believe. That gap has not closed.

### Nell Weaver’s Two Reasons

Nell Weaver was framed in the charm scheme and later shown the fox-like creature. Whether she sees herself as victim, witness, holder of leverage, or future ally remains unresolved.

### Fenwick’s Household Leak

Fenwick’s mother knows more than the group wanted. The cover-up did not stay among children. It reached a parent, and that parent was not fooled.

### Elsie Brook’s Curiosity

Elsie followed Moss through the dark and found the group’s secret. She is observant, connected to Nell, and now holds knowledge that can travel further than the children intend.

### Mother Yela’s Suspicion

Mother Yela knows the children lied for balm. She may not know about the glowing fox-like creature, but she knows enough to watch them more closely.

### The Fox’s Promise

The fox-like creature left with an impression of thanks and a suggestion it would return. Whether that was gratitude, obligation, instinct, or something stranger remains open.

### The Trap

The fox’s leg looked like it had been caught in a snapping trap. Someone set that trap, and the children do not yet know who or why.

### Sickness Beyond Amble

Marek Voss spoke of sickness affecting villages to the north and west. It is distant for now, but it is already disrupting supplies.

### The Guild and the Missing Son

A farmer’s son joined the Guild because of debt and has not been heard from. Marek’s refusal or inability to explain leaves the Guild feeling less like an opportunity and more like a door that closes behind people.

### Wren’s Mother’s Declining Health

During the years that follow, Wren’s mother grows seriously ill. The first hints were already there: tiredness, weakness, grief, and the children not knowing what to say.
